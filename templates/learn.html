What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers attempt to steal sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, credit card details, or other personal data by disguising themselves as trustworthy entities in digital communications.

These attacks typically come through:

  • Emails (most common)
  • Text messages (SMS phishing or "smishing")
  • Voice calls (voice phishing or "vishing")
  • Social media messages
  • Fraudulent websites

‚ö†Ô∏è The Danger of Phishing

Phishing remains one of the most effective attack vectors because it exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Even users with strong technical knowledge can fall victim if they're not vigilant.

Common Phishing Techniques

Phishers use a variety of techniques to trick victims into taking actions that compromise their security:

Attackers create URLs that look legitimate but actually lead to fraudulent websites. They use several methods:

  • Typosquatting: Using domains with common typos (amazom.com instead of amazon.com)
  • Lookalike domains: Using similar-looking characters (paypaI.com with a capital I instead of paypal.com)
  • Subdomain tricks: Using subdomains to confuse users (account-paypal.com instead of paypal.com)
https://secure-paypal.malicious-site.com/accounts/verification?token=123456

In the example above, the actual domain is malicious-site.com, not PayPal.

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging information. Common tactics include:

  • Authority: Impersonating trusted figures like IT support, executives, or government agencies
  • Urgency: Creating time pressure to force quick decisions without proper verification
  • Fear: Threatening negative consequences (account suspension, legal action)
  • Curiosity: Baiting victims with intriguing content ("See who viewed your profile")
  • Familiarity: Posing as friends, colleagues, or known brands

Attackers create fake websites that mimic legitimate ones to steal login credentials or payment information.

  • Copied logos, layouts, and content from legitimate websites
  • Fake login forms that send data to attackers
  • Slight visual differences that might go unnoticed

Example: A fake login page for your bank that looks almost identical to the real one, but the URL is different, and your credentials get sent to attackers.

Attackers forge email headers to make messages appear to come from trusted sources.

  • Makes the "From" field show a trusted organization or individual
  • Can sometimes bypass basic email filters
  • Often combined with content that mimics legitimate communications

Example: An email that appears to be from "support@microsoft.com" but was actually sent by an attacker.

Attackers attach harmful files to emails that, when opened, install malware on the victim's system.

  • Common malicious file types: .exe, .zip, .js, .vbs, .doc/.docm with macros
  • Often disguised as invoices, shipping notices, or receipts
  • May contain ransomware, keyloggers, or remote access trojans

Warning: Never open attachments from unknown senders, and be very cautious even with known senders if you weren't expecting an attachment.

Protecting Yourself from Phishing

While no solution is perfect, these best practices can significantly reduce your risk of falling victim to phishing attacks:

üîç

Verify Sender Identity

Check the full email address, not just the display name. Look for subtle misspellings or different domains.

üñ±Ô∏è

Hover Before Clicking

Hover over links to see where they actually lead. Never click if the destination looks suspicious.

üîê

Use Multi-Factor Authentication

Enable MFA/2FA on all accounts that support it to add an extra layer of security beyond passwords.

üö´

Be Skeptical of Urgency

Be extra cautious with messages creating urgency or threatening consequences if you don't act immediately.

üåê

Go Directly to Websites

Instead of clicking links, manually navigate to the official website by typing the URL yourself.

üîÑ

Keep Software Updated

Regularly update your operating system, browsers, and security software to protect against known vulnerabilities.

üìû

Verify via Official Channels

When in doubt, contact the organization directly using their official phone number or website (not from the suspicious message).

üîë

Use Unique, Strong Passwords

Create different strong passwords for each account and consider using a password manager.

üí° Pro Tip: The STOP Method

When you receive a suspicious message:

  • Stop and think before acting
  • Take time to check the sender and links
  • Opt to go directly to websites instead of clicking links
  • Protect your information by never sharing sensitive details via email
Advanced Security Measures

Modern email services offer several security features to help identify phishing:

  • Spam filters: Automatically detect and quarantine suspicious emails
  • DMARC/SPF/DKIM: Email authentication protocols that verify sender legitimacy
  • Security warnings: Alerts about suspicious content, attachments, or senders
  • External email tags: Labels that mark emails coming from outside your organization

Make sure spam filtering is enabled in your email settings, and periodically check your spam folder for legitimate messages that may have been incorrectly filtered.

Modern browsers include features to help identify and block phishing websites:

  • Safe Browsing: Built-in features that warn about known malicious sites
  • HTTPS indicators: Visual cues showing if a connection is secure
  • Anti-phishing extensions: Add-ons that provide additional protection
  • URL filtering: Tools that check links against databases of known threats

Additional software that can help protect against phishing:

  • Antivirus/Anti-malware: Detects and blocks malicious software
  • Email security gateways: Filter out suspicious emails before they reach inboxes
  • Password managers: Generate and store unique passwords and can help avoid autofilling credentials on phishing sites
  • VPNs: Add encryption to your connection and can include additional security features

Example 1: Banking Phishing Email

From: secur1ty@bank.com

Subject: URGENT: Your account has been locked

Date: August 15, 2025

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. To protect your funds, your online banking access has been temporarily suspended.

To restore your account access, please click here to verify your identity and update your information.

If you do not complete this verification within 24 hours, your account will be permanently locked.

Bank Security Team

Why this is phishing: The email uses urgency tactics, has a subtly misspelled sender address, asks you to click a suspicious link, and threatens negative consequences. A real bank would ask you to log in directly to their website (by typing the URL yourself) and wouldn't threaten to lock your account permanently with such a short timeline.

Example 2: Package Delivery Notification

From: delivery-notification@amazon-shipments.com

Subject: Your Amazon package #38721 could not be delivered

Date: August 16, 2025

Hello,

Our courier attempted to deliver your package today, but was unable to complete the delivery due to an addressing issue.

Your package will be returned to our facility unless you confirm your delivery details within 24 hours.

To reschedule your delivery:

Click here to verify your delivery address

Amazon Delivery Services

Why this is phishing: The sender's email domain is not an official Amazon domain. The message creates urgency and a problem that needs immediate action. Amazon would typically send such notifications from amazon.com domains and would not require you to "confirm details" via an email link to avoid a return.

‚ö†Ô∏è Fake Login Page Demo

This is what happens when you click on a phishing link that leads to a fake login page:

  1. You're directed to a page that looks identical to a legitimate service (like Gmail or Facebook)
  2. The website address is slightly different from the real one, but many users don't notice
  3. When you enter your credentials, they're sent directly to the attacker
  4. The page may redirect you to the real website afterward, making you think nothing happened

Result: The attacker now has your username and password and can access your account.

‚ö†Ô∏è Data Entry Form Demo

This is what happens when you click on a phishing link that leads to a data entry form:

  1. You're shown a form that claims to need your personal information (often with a compelling reason)
  2. The form requests excessive personal details like:
    • Full name, address, phone number
    • Date of birth, Social Security Number
    • Credit card details including CVV code
  3. All information entered is sent directly to the attacker

Result: The attacker collects enough information to commit identity theft or financial fraud.

‚ö†Ô∏è Malicious Download Demo

This is what happens when you click on a phishing link that triggers a malware download:

  1. The link initiates an automatic download of a malicious file
  2. The file may be disguised as a PDF, invoice, or other innocent document
  3. If you open it, malware is installed on your device without visible signs
  4. Depending on the type of malware, it might:
    • Record your keystrokes to steal passwords
    • Encrypt your files and demand ransom (ransomware)
    • Give hackers remote access to your device
    • Spy on your activities or steal your files

Result: Your device is compromised, potentially leading to data theft, financial loss, or privacy violations.

‚ö†Ô∏è Credential Harvester Demo

This is what happens when you encounter a credential harvesting attack:

  1. You're presented with a page that may first ask for one set of credentials
  2. After entering those, you're asked for additional credentials for "verification"
  3. The page may request access to multiple services:
    • Email account
    • Cloud storage
    • Social media
    • Banking information
  4. All credentials are collected by the attacker

Result: The attacker gains access to multiple accounts, increasing the damage potential and making recovery more difficult.
